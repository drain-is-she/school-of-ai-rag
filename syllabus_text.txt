Program Overview 
Artificial Intelligence (AI) aims to imbue machines with human-like capabilities such as 
sensing, comprehension, and action. These technologies are rapidly evolving and reshaping 
economies and societies worldwide. As the demand for AI applications grows, there is a 
pressing need to prepare a skilled workforce for the future. At the same time, the wide-spread 
interest of humanity in the development of AI entails technologies that are state-of-the-art today 
will be rendered deprecated and obsolete soon shortly, demanding that the aspects of application 
and fundamental research are approached in synergistic tandem, where the demands of 
application catalyze research in the fundamental domain, while at the same time, innovation in 
fundamental research aspects of AI fuel the growth of applications. 
It is with the above vision and recognition that the B. Tech program in Artificial Intelligence at 
Bennett University seeks to nurture the next generation of students, who will have the potential 
to revolutionize the AI landscape through rigorous training in the aspects of fundamental 
research and application, for more inclusive and impactful development of society at large. 
Besides the major subject, the students will have the option of pursuing a professional 
specializations in a range of relevant disciplines where the advent of AI is already making a 
meaningful impact, or yet to make inroads, such as media, law, finance, signal processing, 
networks, environmental sustainability, psychology, biotech pharma, materials, agriculture, 
digital healthcare, data science, quantum information processing, and robotics, thereby 
preparing them to tackle the challenges faced by the different industrial and academic domains. 
2 
Objectives 
The aim and scope of the B. Tech program in Artificial Intelligence (AI) is to equip and prepare 
students with comprehensive knowledge of the fundamental mathematical and computational 
aspects of AI to drive the next generation of novel research in the domain, while simultaneously 
accruing practical skills in the current state-of-the-art AI technologies. Through a blend of 
theoretical understanding and hands-on experience, graduates will be prepared for impactful 
careers across various industries and research domains. They will possess the flexibility to 
explore diverse fields such as Defense, Finance Technology (Fintech), Healthcare, 
Agriculture/Food Processing, Education, Retail/Customer Engagement, Human-Robot 
Interaction/Intelligent Automation, Smart City development, and Accessibility Technology for 
the differently abled. 
3 
 
Expected Graduate Attributes 
Upon completion of this program, students will develop the following skills: 
1. Understand foundational concepts and gain practical experience with state-of-the-art 
AI methodologies. 
2. Design, implement, and deploy machine learning applications using structured and 
unstructured data types (e.g., speech, text, images/videos). 
3. Evaluate the reliability, dependability, and ethical considerations of AI systems. 
4. Develop AI systems for complex planning, decision-making, and learning, 
addressing domain-specific challenges with reasoned solutions. 
5. Create AI applications optimized for resource-constrained environments. 
6. Adhere to ethical guidelines and privacy regulations across diverse domains and 
regions. 
7. Plan and manage AI projects efficiently. 
Learning Outcomes: 
Upon completing the B. Tech in Artificial Intelligence, students will: 
1. Possess a deeper understanding of the mathematical and statistical aspects that form 
the fundamental pillars of AI to unravel the “black box” nature of AI to develop 
responsible and cognizable AI frameworks. 
2. Master essential AI concepts, including Machine Learning, Inference Engines, Speech 
Recognition, Computer Vision, Natural Language Understanding, Robotics, and 
Human-Computer Interaction. 
3. Integrate human cognition, AI, and data engineering knowledge to design holistic 
systems. 
4. Possess the understanding of fundamental aspects of AI to drive research towards the 
development of next-generation AI tools. 
5. Develop real-time, robust AI-based systems tailored to specific software, hardware, 
and data constraints.

Program Objectives 
1. PO1: Engineering knowledge: Apply the knowledge of mathematics, science, 
engineering fundamentals, and a professional specialization to solve complex real-world 
problems.  
2. PO2: Problem analysis: Identify, formulate, review research literature, and analyze 
complex real-world problems, reaching substantiated conclusions using first principles 
of mathematics, natural sciences, management, media, and engineering sciences.  
3. PO3: Design/development of solutions: Design solutions for complex real-world 
problems and design system components or processes that meet the specified needs with 
appropriate considerations for public health and safety, as well as cultural, societal, and 
environmental concerns.  
4. PO4: Conduct investigations of complex problems: Use research-based knowledge and 
research methods, including design of experiments, analysis and interpretation of data, 
and synthesis of the information to provide valid conclusions.  
5. PO5: Modern tool usage: Create, select, and apply appropriate techniques, resources, 
and modern engineering and IT tools, including prediction and modeling, to complex 
engineering activities with an understanding of the limitations.  
6. PO6: The engineer and society: Apply reasoning informed by contextual knowledge to 
assess societal, health, safety, legal, and cultural issues and the consequent 
responsibilities relevant to professional engineering practice.   
7. PO7: Environment and sustainability: Understand the impact of professional 
engineering solutions in societal and environmental contexts and demonstrate the 
knowledge of and need for sustainable development.  
8. PO8: Ethics: Apply ethical principles and commit to professional ethics, responsibilities, 
and norms of engineering practice.  
9. PO9: Individual and teamwork: Function effectively as an individual and as a member 
or leader in diverse teams and in multidisciplinary settings.  
10. PO10: Communication: Communicate effectively on complex engineering activities 
with the engineering community and with society at large, such as being able to 
comprehend and write effective reports and design documentation, make effective 
presentations, and give and receive clear instructions.  
7 
11. PO11: Project management and finance: Demonstrate knowledge and understanding 
of the engineering and management principles and apply these to one’s work as a member 
and leader in a team to manage projects and in multidisciplinary environments.  
12. PO12: Life-long learning: Recognize the need for and have the preparation and ability 
to engage in independent and life-long learning in the broadest context of technological 
change.  
8 
Program-Specific Learning Outcomes 
By the end of this degree program, students should have the following knowledge, skills, and 
attitudes: 
1. Equip students with professional competencies to excel independently and collaboratively, 
nurturing problem-solving abilities and effective communication skills through diverse 
pedagogical approaches. 
2. Apply domain-specific knowledge in Artificial Intelligence for analysis and design, crafting 
tailored solutions for the AI industry, and fostering entrepreneurial initiatives in AI-related 
ventures. 
3. Utilize acquired technical expertise in AI across diverse sectors, fostering the generation of 
innovative ideas and solutions in rapidly evolving fields such as Digital Healthcare, Agri
Tech, Fin-Tech, Legal-Tech, Quantum Information Processing, Bio-Tech, Signal Processing, 
Data Science, Media-Tech, Digital Marketing, and more.

ABAI1001L 
 
 
 
Course Outcomes (COs) 
On completion of this course, the students will be able to: 
 
CO1: Understand basic concepts of systems of linear equations, vector space, linear transformations, 
and matrix representation of a linear transformation with application to computer graphics. 
CO2: Determine eigenvalues and eigenvectors and solve eigenvalue problems. 
CO3: Analyse and apply the notions of continuity and differentiability to algebraic and 
transcendental functions. 
CO4: Analyse and apply the concepts of maxima & minima in engineering problems.

Course Contents: 
 
Module I: (Linear Algebra)                                                                           21 Lecture Hours 
Systems of linear equations, matrices, Gaussian elimination, echelon form, column space, null 
space, the rank of a matrix, inverse and determinant; Vector spaces (over the field of real and 
complex numbers), subspaces, spanning set, linear independence, basis, and dimension; Linear 
transformations, rank-nullity theorem, matrix of a linear transformation, change of basis and 
similarity; Eigenvalues and eigenvectors, algebraic and geometric multiplicity, diagonalization 
by similarity; Inner-product spaces, Gram-Schmidt process, orthonormal basis; Orthogonal, 
Hermitian and symmetric matrices, spectral theorem for real symmetric matrices, Singular value 
decomposition (Overview)  
 
Module II: (One Variable Calculus)                                                            24 Lecture Hours 
Convergence of sequences and series of real numbers; Limits and Continuity - definition and 
examples, Intermediate Value Property (IVP), Derivative of a function, Differentiation rules, 
L'Hôpital's Rule, Chain rule, Implicit differentiation, Local maxima and Local minima, 
Intermediate Value theorem, Rolle’s Theorem and Mean Value Theorem. Functions and their 
Geometric properties; Convexity, Concavity, and Curve sketching; Improper integrals; 
Application to Length, Area, Volume, and Surface area of revolution.

Course Outcomes (COs) 
On completion of this course, the students will be able to: 
  
CO1: Get knowledge of basic electronics elements, devices & theorems. 
CO2: Develop an understanding of designing a digital logic circuit-based system to achieve a prescribed task. 
CO3: Analyse and interpret digital circuits and develop an ability to conduct experiments 
CO4: Analyse and synthesize logic networks using both traditional techniques and modern CAD tools. 
CO5: Investigate the state diagrams and form the logic circuits for them.

Course Contents: 
Module 1                    8 Lecture Hours 
Basic Circuit Theory: Kirchoff’s current law (KCL) and Kirchhoff’s voltage law (KVL), Nodal analysis and 
Mesh analysis, Network theorems: Thevenin’s theorem, Norton’s theorem, Maximum power transfer theorem, 
and Superposition theorem, Introduction to the capacitor (C) & the inductor (L), introduction to phasor 
diagram, series and parallel combination of inductors, Impedance and frequency dependency, RL and RC filter 
circuits (low pass, high pass), transfer function, Diodes and its applications. 
Module 2                    8 Lecture Hours 
Number systems and digital logic: Number system, decimal, hexadecimal and binary numbers Digital gates: OR, 
AND, NOT, NOR, Ex-OR, Ex-NOR. Boolean algebra, Simplification of Boolean expressions, the realization of 
Boolean expressions using logic gates, Minimization Techniques: Boolean postulates and laws, De-Morgan’s 
Theorem, Principle of Duality - Boolean expression, Minimization of Boolean expressions, Minterm, Maxterm, 
Sum of Products (SOP), Product of Sums (POS), Karnaugh map Minimization, Don’t care conditions,  Logic 
Gates, Implementations of Logic Functions using gates. 
 
Module 3                   
7 Lecture Hours 
Combinational circuits, Design procedure, Half and full adders, Half and full subtractors, Parallel binary adder 
and subtractor, Fast Adder, Carry Look Ahead adder, Serial Adder/Subtractor, BCD adder, Binary Multiplier, 
Binary Divider, Multiplexer/Demultiplexer, decoder/encoder, Parity checker, parity generators, code converters, 
magnitude Comparator 
Module 4                  
7 Lecture Hours 
Latches, Flip-flops, Master-slave flip-flop, Characteristic table and equation, Edge triggering, Level 
Triggering, Realization of one flip flop using other flip flops, Serial adder/subtractor, Counters: Asynchronous 
and synchronous, up/down, Modulo-n, Design of synchronous circuits: state diagram, state table, state 
minimization, state assignment, shift registers. 
List of experiments:  
1. Implementation of Ohm’s law, Kirchoff’s current law and voltage law 
2. Implementation of theorems 
3. Low pass and high pass Filters (using Resistor and Capacitor). 
4. Current-Voltage characteristics of a PN Junction Diode (Ge and Si), Implementation of Half & Full wave 
rectifier 
5. Logic behaviour of various IC-based logic gates. (IC-7400, 7408, 7486) 
6. Combinational circuit design using decoders (IC 74155 Dual-2*4 decoder and   external NAND GATES.) 
7. Mid-term Assessment 
8. Adders and Subtractors design (AND, OR, EXOR GATE IC-7408, 7432, 7486) 
9. Design Multiplexers and Demultiplexers (IC 74151, 74155, 7493) 
10. Design Flip-flops (IC 7474, 7476, & LED Strip) 
11. Operation of a 4-bit binary counter (Mode-16 & mode-10 counters using IC   7400, 7408, 7486, 7493, & 
LED strip) 
12. Counters (asynchronous/ripple and synchronous) work demonstration 
13. Design Up/Down counters & Shift registers (IC -74195 & LED strips) 
14. End-term Assessment

Course Outcomes (COs) 
On completion of this course, the students will be able to: 
 
CO1: Develop a solid understanding of basic probability theory and its applications 
CO2: Learn methods of statistical inference for making conclusions based on data. 
CO3: Understand how to model data and interpret statistical results. 
CO4: Develop critical thinking skills in statistical reasoning and problem-solving. 
CO5: Gain proficiency in using statistical software for data analysis.

Course Contents: 
 
Module I:                                         10 Lecture Hours 
Basic probability rules and axioms, Combinatorics and counting techniques, Conditional Probability, 
Independence, Law of total probability, Bayes’ theorem, Random variables, and probability distributions 
(Discrete), Random variables and probability distributions (Continuous), Expected value variance, 
moments, Joint Probability, Joint Conditional Probability Correlation, Covariance, Transformations of 
random variables. 
Discrete Distributions, Probability Mass Function (PMF); Continuous Distribution, Probability density 
function (PDF), Cumulative distribution function (CDF).  
 
Module II:                                     15 Lecture Hours 
Discrete Distributions, Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson, Geometric, Applications of Discrete Distribution, 
Continuous Distribution: Uniform Distribution, Normal Distribution, Standard normal distributions, Z 
Scores, Z tables, Exponential, applications of distributions. 
Sampling distribution, Sampling distribution of the sample mean, Sampling distribution of the sample 
proportion, Central Limit Theorem (CLT), Law of Large Numbers (LLN), Standard error of a statistic, 
Sampling distribution of the difference between two means, Sampling distribution of the difference between 
two proportions. 
Module III:                     10 Lecture Hours  
Sampling distribution of sample variance, t-distribution, Chi-Square distribution, F-distribution, Finite 
population correction factor, Effect of sample size on sampling distribution, Bootstrapping and resampling 
methods, Bias and unbiased estimators, Sampling distribution in Bayesian inference, Order statistics and their 
distributions. Confidence Interval Estimation, Known sigma and unknown sigma based estimation, Markov 
Inequality, Chebyshev inequality.  Hypothesis Testing, Z Test, T Test, F test, ANOVA, AB Testing.   
Module IV:                                       
10 Lecture Hours 
Parameter estimation, Linear regression, multiple regression, Least Squares Estimation (LSE), and 
Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE), Maximum A Posteriori (MAP), Markov chains, Statistical 
Inference, Conformal Prediction. 
List of Experiments: 
1. Simulate and analyze discrete distributions (Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson) using their PMFs to compute expected 
values and variances. 
2. Visualize transformations of random variables and their impact on distributions, including normal and 
exponential distributions. 
3. Apply Bayes' Theorem to update probabilities in a medical diagnostic scenario using prior and conditional 
probabilities.  
4. Simulate and compare the sampling distributions of the sample mean and sample proportion for a binomial 
population.  
5. Visualize the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) by simulating sample means for different sample sizes and 
distributions.  
6. Examine the standard normal distribution, Z-scores, and their applications in hypothesis testing using real-world 
data.  
7. Compare confidence intervals for two population proportions using simulation-based sampling distributions. 
8. Estimate the sampling distribution of sample variance under different sample sizes using bootstrapping. 
9. Conduct an A/B test on click-through rates using a Z-test and interpret the results with hypothesis testing. 
10. Perform linear and multiple regression using Least Squares Estimation and interpret model coefficients. 
11. Estimate parameters using MLE and MAP for normal and Bernoulli distributions and compare results. 
12. Apply conformal prediction to a regression model and evaluate coverage of prediction intervals.

exploitation tradeoff.

Course Contents: 
Module I: Introduction                                                                                        9 Lecture Hours 
Overview of Image Processing Systems, Digital Image Representation, Components of an 
Image Processing System, Sampling and Quantization, Representing Digital Images (Data 
structure), Some Basic Relationships Between Pixels- Neighbors and Connectivity of pixels 
in image, Applications of Image Processing: Medical imaging, Robot vision, Character 
recognition, Remote Sensing 
Module II: Image Transformation                                                                12 Lecture Hours 
Image transforms (Unitary Transform and properties, 2D Fourier Transform, 2D FFT, 
Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT), Properties of DFT, 2D DCT and properties, Walsh
Hadamard Transform, K-L Transform, Principal Component Analysis (PCA), Wavelet 
transform (Definition, Properties, Mathematical function, Mother wavelets) 
Module III: Image Enhancement and Restoration                                         12 Lecture Hours 
Image Enhancements: Point processing functions, Piecewise linear functions, Histogram methods 
(histogram equalization, specification, and modification), and Bit extraction. Restoration (in spatial 
domain): Image restoration and degradation model, Noise types (Gaussian, Rayleigh, Poisson, other) 
and their pdfs (Probability Distribution Functions), Averaging Filter (Mean Filters (Arithmetic, 
Geometric & Harmonic), Inverse filtering, Wiener Filter, Tikhonov Regularization, LMMSE filters, 
constrained least squares filters, Other related optimization problems                                                                
Module IV: Image Segmentation and Compression                                       
12 Lecture Hours 
Introduction, Detection of isolated points, line detection, Edge detection, Edge linking, 
Region-based segmentation- Region growing, split and merge technique, local processing, 
regional processing, Hough transform, Segmentation using Threshold. Introduction, coding 
Redundancy, Inter-pixel redundancy, image compression model, Lossy and Lossless 
compression, Huffman Coding, Arithmetic Coding, LZW coding, Transform Coding, Sub
image size selection, blocking, DCT implementation using FFT, Run length coding. Dilation, 
Erosion, & other complex operations. 
List of Experiments:  
Analyze the effects of different sampling rates and quantization levels on image quality. 
Explore pixel connectivity and neighborhood relationships in a digital image. Investigate the 
use of image processing techniques in medical imaging. Apply the 2D Fourier Transform to 
an image and analyze the frequency components. Use the wavelet transform to compress an 
image and evaluate the results. Perform histogram equalization on an image and assess the 
enhancement. Apply various filtering techniques to reduce noise in an image and compare 
their effectiveness. Implement edge detection algorithms and analyze their performance on 
different images. Use region-growing techniques for image segmentation and evaluate the 
results. Compare lossy and lossless compression methods on an image and analyze the 
differences in quality and file size. Perform morphological operations.
